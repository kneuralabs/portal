Q.01What is AI governance and why does it matter now?+
AI governance is the framework of policies, controls, and accountability structures that determine how AI systems are owned, monitored, and audited within an organisation. It matters now because regulators, enterprise clients, and procurement teams are actively asking for evidence of it. Without a documented governance programme, organisations face contract losses, regulatory exposure, and liability that cannot be quantified.
Q.02Does the EU AI Act apply to U.S. companies?+
Yes. The Act applies to any organisation deploying AI to users in the EU, regardless of where the organisation is incorporated. U.S. companies whose software or services reach European users — or whose enterprise clients are themselves subject to the Act — need to understand their obligations. Geography is not the qualifier most U.S. companies assume it is.
Q.03What is NIST AI RMF and is alignment mandatory?+
The NIST AI Risk Management Framework is a voluntary federal framework for identifying, assessing, and managing AI risk. It is not currently mandatory for most organisations, but it is the de facto reference standard that auditors, enterprise procurement teams, and federal contractors use to evaluate whether an AI programme is defensible. Alignment is increasingly a commercial requirement rather than a compliance one.
Q.04How long does a governance engagement take?+
Engagements range from 2–20 weeks. A standalone AI Risk Assessment (S1) takes 2–4 weeks. A Governance Framework Setup (S2) takes 4–8 weeks. A complete programme from first inventory to audit-ready certification readiness takes 10–20 weeks. Every timeline is set transparently in the engagement letter before work begins.
Q.05What is ISO/IEC 42001 and should we pursue certification?+
ISO/IEC 42001 is the international standard for AI management systems. Certification is increasingly written into enterprise RFPs and vendor qualification requirements. Whether it is worth pursuing depends on your client base, regulatory exposure, and organisational maturity. We give you an honest assessment of that in the discovery call.
Q.06Who typically owns AI governance inside the company?+
It varies. Often Risk, Legal, or the CIO/CTO function sponsors it; operational ownership commonly sits with a cross-functional AI council. Part of S2 is recommending an ownership model that fits your size, sector, and risk profile — and writing it down so it survives staff turnover.
