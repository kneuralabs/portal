Most AI Governance Programmes Are Designed to Be Filed, Not Used.

Most governance documents have a publication date and a last-reviewed date. Between those two dates, nothing happened. They were written to satisfy a question, not to govern a system. Mid-market AI governance fails for the same reason — it is designed for the moment of completion, not for the years of operation that follow.

The NIST AI RMF and ISO/IEC 42001 are not designed to be completed. They are designed to be operated. That distinction changes who owns governance, what it costs to maintain, and whether it survives contact with a real audit or a real incident. Organisations that build governance as a document usually find out at the worst possible time.

The companies getting this right are not the ones with the most sophisticated policies. They are the ones that mapped every AI system before writing a single policy, assigned real ownership to real people, and built monitoring into the workflow rather than the annual review calendar. That is structural governance. That is what holds.
